Monarchs

Britain

Henry VII - 1485-1509, New Monarch in England

Henry VIII - 1509-1547, Church of England

  • Created Anglican Church so he could divorce his wives that couldn't bear him a male heir
  • Catholic but part of Anglican Church

Edward VI - 1547-1553, weak and sickly son

  • Allowed for Protestant reforms

Mary I - 1553-1558, Catholic

  • "Bloody Mary"
  • Married to Phillip II of Spain. Unpopular because of this.

Elizabeth I - 1558-1603, balanced power between Protestants and Catholics

  • Politique
  • Dealt with Phillip II's Armada

James I - 1603-1625, divine right of kings, created tensions for English Civil War

  • Believed that Presbyterian system would destroy royal control of the Anglican Church

Charles I - 1625-1649, deposed and beheaded in 1649 because fought with Parliament, created forces for English Civil War

  • Son of James I
  • Believed in Divine Right
  • Opposed Puritans and supported Anglican Church
  • For money, Charles I agree to Petitions of Right (1628): Parliament decides taxes, and all should have due process of law
  • Also, Archbishop of Canterbury-- William Laud-- tried to turn Anglican Church Catholic and forced the English Prayer Book on the people. He was supported by Charles I. It didn't work, and when financial crises called Parliament back they executed Laud and limited parliamentary power.

Oliver Cromwell - 1649-1658, Interregnum, Lord Protector

  • Roundhead
  • Favored Parliamentary monarchy; Presbyterian Church with elected Presbyters/ Elders
  • Executed Charles I in 1649 with New Model Army
  • Abolished monarchy and House of Lords

Charles II - 1660-1685, restored English monarchy, was an effective ruler, created balance of power with Parliament. Invited to power.

  • Restoration restored: monarchy, Anglican Church, and Parliament
  • Catholic with no kids, so James II got throne (brother)

James II - 1685-1688, Catholic, tried to force toleration and was deposed, Glorious Revolution

  • Whigs oppose. Tories support monarchy.
  • Wanted to return England to Catholicism, both parties hated him for it.
  • After realising that everyone hated him, he fled to France.

William and Mary - 1689-1702, after Glorious Revolution, accepted Bill of Rights

  • William Of Orange
  • Had to Accept Bill Of Rights (1689) in order to rule.
  • Bill Of Rights meant that English reject Divine Right, and turned England into Constitutional Monarchy

France

Henry IV - 1589-1610, Henry of Navarre, settles Wars of Religion with Edict of Nantes (1598)

  • Edict of Nantes (1598) gave religous toleration to French Huguenots. Made Henry IV a politique.
  • Pay and be a noble system called being a "Noble of the Robe". No titles but still a member of Bourgeoisie.

Louis XIII - 1610-1643, begins French absolutism, *Cardinal Richelieu

Louis XIV - 1643-1715, Sun King, *Colbert

Louis XV - 1715-1774, continues French absolutism, ruled during Enlightenment

Papacy

Pope Leo X - 1513-1521, Renaissance Pope, Michelangelo, symbol of excesses of Renaissance popes

Pope Paul III - 1534-1549, Counter Reformation, Council of Trent

Pope Alexander VI - 1492-1503, Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)

Spain

Charles V - 1519-1556, Global Monarch (King of Spain and HRE), fought against the Ottomans and the Reformation

Philip II - 1556-1598, monarch during height of Spain, sent Spanish Armada

Prince Henry - 1394-1460, the Navigator, Exploration, maritime advance

Austria

Maria Theresa - 1740-1780, set up Austria as an effective state

Joseph II - 1780-1790, Enlightened absolutist, set up Austria as an effective state

Holy Roman Empire (HRE)

Charles V - 1519-1556, HRE, had power because of holdings (half of Europe, i.e. King of Spain), Habsburg, Peace of Augsburg (1555)

Charles VI - 1711-1740, Pragmatic Sanction

Russia

Ivan III - 1462-1505, consolidates Muscovite princes, gets rid of Mongols

Ivan IV - 1583-1594, the Terrible, first Tsar, consolidated monarchy

Peter the Great - 1689-1725, builds Russian absolutist state, westernizes Russian nobility

Catherine the Great - 1762-1796, Enlightened absolutist, division of Poland

Michael Romanov - 1613-1645, establishes Romanov dynasty, is elected to power

Prussia

Frederick William - 1640-1688, the Great Elector, takes Prussia from a minor state to an effective, powerful state, joins Brandenburg and Prussia, gets King title for descendents

Frederick William I - 1713-1740, Soldier King

Frederick the Great - 1740-1786, Enlightened absolutist, uses army to double the size of Prussia

Important Events

Spanish Armada - This battle was fought in the English Channel in 1588, and as a result of the English victory, Spain entered a long period of political, economic, and social decline.

War of the Spanish Succession - The Treaty of Utrecht which ended this war prevented the unification of the French and Spanish thrones under a single monarch.

Glorious Revolution - As a result of this conflict in England, the political philosophy of John Locke was embraced, and a constitutional monarchy was confirmed by William and Mary.

the Fronde - This revolt in France, resulted in Louis XIV's distrust of the nobility, and over the course of his rule he gradually reduced noble power in France.

the Continental System - This was Napoleon's failed attempt to isolate the English economy from trade with French controlled Europe. It was broken by the Russians causing Napoleon to invade in 1812.

St. Bartholomew's Day - This event exemplified the religious violence in France in the wake of the Protestant Reformation.

Pugachev's Revolt - This revolt in Russia led Catherine the Great to abandon the enlightened principles she hoped to institute in her country.

the Great Schism - This crisis in the Roman Catholic Church led to first two and then three sitting Popes. It damaged papal prestige.

Council of Trent - The Catholic church's response to the issues raised by the Protestant reformation were formulated at this meeting held in 1540.

Peace of Augsburg - In 1555, this treaty ended the religious violence in the Holy Roman Empire and established the doctrine of cuius regio, eius religio (whose region, his religion).

the Stuart Restoration - After the death of Cromwell, this event reestablished royal power in England.

Treaty of Westphalia - This treaty ended the Thirty Years' War and resulted in the recognition of Calvinism. It also confirmed the independence of the United Provinces of the Netherlands and the Swiss Confederation.

Civill Constitution of the Clergy - During the moderate phase of the French Revolution, this governmental arrangement with the French Church made clergymen little more than employees of the state.

Pragmatic Sanction - This was issued by Charles VI of Austria and declared that Habsburg lands were to remain intact regardless of who inherited the throne.

Edict of Nantes - This was issued by France's Henry IV (Henry of Navarre) in order to reestablish order after the War of the Three Henrys. It granted limited toleration to the Huguenot minority in France.

Reign of Terror - Robespierre is most closely associated with this period of the French Revolution-a period during which the guillotine was used to execute royalists, moderates, and other enemies of the regime.

German Peasants' Revolt - As a result of this violent event, Martin Luther wrote Against the Murdering Thieving Hordes of Peasants.

Enclosure - This event/trend in England resulted in the peasants' loss of access to common land, and as a result, many peasants became landless cottagers.

Babylonian Captivity - This is the name given to the period of time during which the papacy took up residence in Avignon.

English Civil War - This English conflict was fought between the forces of the king and the forces of Parliament. As a result of the conflict, Charles I was executed and Oliver Cromwell established a protectorate over England

Price Revolution - This event/trend occurred during the latter stage of the European Age of Discovery and was the result of high inflation brought on by population increase and an influx of silver from the "new world."

carnival - This annual celebration preceded Lent, and during the early modern period it was characterized by revelry and excess.

Tennis Court Oath - This event of the moderate phase of the French Revolution resulted in the creation of a national assembly that included members of all three estates of the French realm.

the Dutch Revolt - Although this conflict was, on the surface, a war of religion, it had both political and economic overtones. As a result of the conflict, Spain lost control of a wealthy region and Amsterdam became Europe's center of banking and commerce for a brief period of time.

the Black Death - This arrived in Europe in 1342 at the port city of Messina, and by the time it ran its course, Europe lost between one-third and one-half of its population.
